# Essay Concepts — Private Answer Key

This is the tutor's reference. **Never paste definitions or answers from here into chat or lesson files** — translate them into questions. Verify the student's reasoning against this key.

## Argument anatomy

- **Claim** — a statement a reasonable, informed person could disagree with. Not a fact (settled by checking), not a preference (settled by nobody — it's just yours).
- **Reason** — answers *"why should I believe the claim?"* A claim usually needs 2+ independent reasons.
- **Evidence** — answers *"how do you know the reason holds?"* Facts, numbers, studies, expert statements, verifiable events.
- **Counterargument** — the strongest opposing case, stated fairly (steelman).
- **Rebuttal** — concede what's true, then show why your side still wins ("It's true that…, but…").
- **Thesis** — the claim, sharpened for an essay: debatable + specific + supportable. Often previews the reasons.

The quick test chain: *disagree-test* (is it a claim?) → *why* (reasons) → *how do you know* (evidence) → *who disagrees and what's their best shot* (counter/rebuttal).

## Evidence quality ladder (strongest → weakest)

1. **Systematic data** — studies, surveys with real sample sizes ("a 2023 survey of 1,200 schools…").
2. **Expert testimony** — named, relevantly credentialed, ideally explaining *why* ("Dr. Reyes, who studies how teens learn…").
3. **Verifiable specific facts** — checkable numbers and events ("eleven bags a day", "$4,800 saved in Riverdale").
4. **Personal anecdote** — one real but unsystematic experience ("one time I…"). Vivid, weak alone; fine as seasoning.
5. **Hearsay / vague consensus** — "my cousin says", "everyone knows". Fails every quality question.

Quality questions: **Who says so?** (named? qualified?) · **How many?** (one story or a pattern?) · **Could I check it?** (verifiable?) · **Is it recent/relevant?**

Lesson 3 ranking note: B > D > (A/E/C in some order) is the canonical shape. B is systematic; D is expert; A is hearsay; E is anecdote; C is vague consensus dressed as fact. A defensible swap (e.g. E above A because at least it's first-hand) is fine **if argued with the quality questions** — the reasoning is the lesson, not the exact order.

## Counterargument moves

- **Steelman, never strawman** — argue the other side as its smartest believer would. For "homework should be banned on weekends": the steelman is spaced practice/retention and preparing for deadlines, NOT "teachers just like assigning it."
- **Concede-then-counter** — "It's true that practice matters… but practice can be scheduled on weekdays; what weekends uniquely provide (rest, family, recovery) can't be."
- Why naming the other side helps: it shows the writer has seen the whole board; readers trust writers who don't hide from the hard part. An unanswered objection in the reader's head beats your essay silently.

## Thesis tests

A thesis must be:
1. **Debatable** — fails: "bullying is bad" (nobody disagrees).
2. **Specific** — fails: "phones are an issue in schools" (what about them? where? who?). Passes: "students in grades 6–12 should be allowed phones during breaks, but not in class."
3. **Supportable** — fails: "school is the worst thing ever invented" (debatable and specific-ish, but no honest evidence will carry it).

Topic ≠ announcement ≠ thesis: "phones at school" (topic) → "this essay is about phones at school" (announcement) → "our school should allow phones during breaks because X and Y" (thesis).

## PEEL (body paragraph anatomy)

- **Point** — the reason, one sentence, up front.
- **Evidence** — the support, concrete.
- **Explain** — the bridge from evidence to thesis: *what does this prove and why does it matter?* The most-skipped move; "quote and run" is the classic failure.
- **Link** — a handoff sentence to the next paragraph (or back to the thesis).

A paragraph with P+E but no Explain reads as a fact dump. The Explain move is where the arguing actually happens.

## Hooks (Lesson 6/7)

Works: a surprising fact or number, a sharp question, a 1–2 sentence scene, a bold statement the essay will earn. Fails: "In this essay I will…" (announcement), dictionary definitions, "Since the dawn of time…".

## Capstone rubric (Lesson 7 — verify before celebrating)

1. Intro: hook (not an announcement) + context + thesis as the last sentence.
2. Two body paragraphs, each a complete PEEL; reasons distinct, not the same reason twice in different clothes.
3. Counterargument paragraph: steelman + genuine concession + counter.
4. Conclusion: answers "so what?" — a consequence, a call to action, or the bigger stake. Not a restated intro.
5. Evidence survives the quality questions; at least one piece above anecdote level.
6. At least one revision the student made themselves after the draft (this is the graded skill — the reviser's eye).

## Per-Lesson Answer Notes

- **L1:** "Pizza is my favorite food" = preference (no argument possible — it's about the speaker). "The school day is six hours long" = fact (settled by checking a clock, not arguing). "The school day should be shorter" = claim ("should" is a strong claim signal — but don't teach the shortcut; let them find disagreement-possibility).
- **L2:** Claim = "Our school cafeteria should switch to reusable trays." Reason 1 = trash/waste; Reason 2 = cost. Evidence = 11 bags/day, 2,000 bags/year, $4,800 in Riverdale. The last sentence is a bonus concede-then-counter — gold star if they spot its job (it plants Lesson 4).
- **L3:** see ranking note above.
- **L4:** steelman for weekend-homework ban: retention/spaced practice, time-management skills, course pacing. A concession that gives something real ("It's true that practice helps memory…") is required — "It's true that some people disagree" concedes nothing.
- **L5:** the thesis must be THEIR claim sharpened, not a new topic. If they switch topics, ask what made the first one stop being worth defending (switching is allowed; drifting unexamined is not).
- **L6:** most likely failure is no Explain move — probe with the so-what test. The hook test: read it as a stranger; do you want sentence two?
- **L7:** judge with the rubric above. Require one self-driven revision; ask them to name what they changed and why.
