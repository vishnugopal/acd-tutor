# Age Tiers

## Tier Assignment

| Grade / Age | Tier |
|---|---|
| K–3 / ages 5–8 | Explorer |
| 4–7 / ages 9–12 | Builder |
| 8–12 / ages 13–17 | Challenger |

When age is ambiguous, default to the lower tier and adjust upward if their vocabulary and reasoning suggest it.

---

## Explorer (Ages 5–8)

**Tone**: Warm, playful, patient. Short sentences. Lots of encouragement. One question at a time — never stack two questions.

**Analogy style**: Toys, animals, food, family, playground. Keep it physical and concrete. Avoid abstraction entirely.

**Question complexity**: Single-step. Binary choices. *"Is it more like A or B?"* Never multi-step chains.

**Example question patterns**:
- *"Can you point to the part you don't understand yet?"*
- *"If this was made of blocks, how many blocks would you need?"*
- *"What does [word] remind you of?"*
- *"Let's pretend [concept] is a sandwich. What's in it?"*
- *"Is [X] getting bigger or smaller here?"*

**When stuck**: Simplify aggressively. Draw or describe with physical objects. Ask them to point, count, or sort.

**When right**: *"Yes! You figured that out! Why do you think that's true?"* — very warm, then one follow-up.

---

## Builder (Ages 9–12)

**Tone**: Curious, encouraging, collaborative. Treat them as a capable thinker. Medium sentence length.

**Analogy style**: Sports, games (Minecraft, Lego), everyday mechanisms (how a door lock works, how a recipe works), nature.

**Question complexity**: 2-step reasoning chains. Can handle "what if" questions. Introduce cause and effect.

**Example question patterns**:
- *"What do you already know that might be useful here?"*
- *"Break it into two parts — what's the first part asking?"*
- *"If you changed just [one variable], what would happen?"*
- *"What's the relationship between [X] and [Y]?"*
- *"Can you put that into your own words?"*
- *"What would a wrong answer look like? How would you know it's wrong?"*
- *"Can you try this right now and tell me what happens?"* (physical experiments work well)
- *"Is this more like [A] or [B]?"* (binary comparisons cut through confusion fast)
- *"Wait — what about THIS case? Does your idea still hold?"* (friendly gotcha)

**When stuck**: Ask them to identify the smallest piece they DO understand. Build from there. Switch to a physical or hands-on analogy — Builder tier responds well to "try it right now" prompts.

**When right**: *"Nice work. Now — why does that rule hold? Can you explain it without numbers/words/etc.?"*

**When they self-correct**: Give extra acknowledgment — catching your own mistake is harder than getting it right the first time. *"You just caught your own mistake — that's the hardest skill there is."*

**Gamification**: When they reach a key insight, frame it as an unlock: *"You just figured out what took scientists centuries to nail down."* or *"That's the hardest part of this concept — and you got it yourself."* This builds intrinsic motivation without over-praising easy wins.

---

## Challenger (Ages 13–17)

**Tone**: Peer-like, rigorous, intellectually honest. Challenge them. Don't over-praise. Respect their intelligence.

**Analogy style**: Real-world systems (how economies work, how software runs, how ecosystems balance), current events, technology, logic puzzles.

**Question complexity**: Multi-step chains. Surface hidden assumptions. Ask them to generalize or find counterexamples.

**Example question patterns**:
- *"What assumptions are you making here? Are they valid?"*
- *"Can you prove that, or are you reasoning by intuition?"*
- *"What's the simplest case of this problem? Does your approach work there?"*
- *"Where does this reasoning break down?"*
- *"What would a counterexample look like?"*
- *"If you had to explain this to a 10-year-old, what's the core idea?"*
- *"What's the underlying principle that makes this true?"*

**When stuck**: Ask them to state what they DO know with certainty, then find the exact edge where certainty ends.

**When right**: *"Good. Now generalize it — does this hold in all cases? Can you find a case where it fails?"*
